# Product Lifecycle

# Project Trident Product Lifecycle

---

# Introduction

Products within Project Trident should not be treated as static listings.

Each product should move through a structured lifecycle involving:
- submission,
- visibility,
- validation,
- review,
- and long-term trust evaluation.

This lifecycle helps ensure that:
- new technologies receive fair exposure,
- users understand verification status,
- and recommendations remain transparent.

The lifecycle system is intended to balance:
- innovation visibility,
- uncertainty communication,
- and long-term trust consistency.

---

# Core Lifecycle Philosophy

Project Trident believes that:
- visibility should not depend only on popularity,
- but trust should also not be assumed immediately.

New products deserve:
- fair evaluation,
- contextual visibility,
- and transparent communication.

The lifecycle system exists to:
- explain product maturity,
- clarify verification status,
- and reduce misleading discovery behavior.

---

# High-Level Lifecycle Stages

Products may move through the following stages:

1. Submission
2. Initial Visibility
3. Verification Queue
4. Platform Evaluation
5. Verified Status
6. Long-Term Observation
7. Re-evaluation
8. Archived / Deprecated Status

---

# Stage 1 — Product Submission

Products may initially enter the platform through:
- internal platform research,
- manual catalog entry,
- or future vendor submission systems.

At this stage:
- information is considered unverified,
- and product trust is limited.

The product may contain:
- vendor specifications,
- launch information,
- and initial technology classifications.

---

# Initial Product State

Newly added products should clearly indicate:
- verification has not yet occurred,
- long-term reliability is unknown,
- and platform evaluation may still be pending.

Example status concepts:
- “Unverified”
- “Vendor Claims Only”
- “Under Initial Review”

Transparency during this stage is essential.

---

# Stage 2 — Initial Visibility

Project Trident intentionally allows controlled visibility for:
- new technologies,
- emerging vendors,
- and recent launches.

This differs from traditional marketplaces that heavily prioritize:
- historical momentum,
- review volume,
- and sales history.

However, visibility should always include:
- uncertainty communication,
- and clear trust labeling.

---

# Discovery Behavior During Initial Visibility

Products in early stages may appear in:
- technology discovery sections,
- innovation-focused exploration,
- or “under evaluation” categories.

The platform should avoid:
- presenting unverified products as fully trusted recommendations.

Users should always understand:
- what has been verified,
- and what remains uncertain.

---

# Stage 3 — Verification Queue

Products may enter verification queues based on factors such as:
- technology significance,
- user interest,
- platform priorities,
- or emerging visibility.

Verification capacity may initially remain limited.

Therefore, Project Trident should prioritize:
- high-impact technologies,
- meaningful engineering changes,
- and products with significant long-term implications.

---

# Stage 4 — Platform Evaluation

During evaluation, products may undergo:
- specification verification,
- hands-on testing,
- methodology-driven scoring,
- and reviewer analysis.

Evaluation areas may include:
- durability,
- latency,
- compatibility,
- firmware quality,
- repairability,
- and long-term reliability indicators.

The methodology should remain:
- transparent,
- repeatable,
- and platform-controlled.

---

# Separation of Trust Layers

Throughout evaluation, Project Trident should clearly separate:

## Vendor Claims
Information provided by vendors.

## User Feedback
Community experiences and ownership reports.

## Platform Verification
Independent evaluation performed through platform methodologies.

These layers should never be merged into a misleading single trust signal.

---

# Stage 5 — Verified Status

After evaluation, products may receive statuses such as:
- Verified
- Conditionally Verified
- Not Verified

Verification does not necessarily mean:
- “best product”
or:
- “perfect product.”

It means:
- the platform has independently evaluated the product according to its methodology.

---

# Recommendation Eligibility

Only products reaching sufficient trust maturity should become:
- strongly recommended,
- prominently surfaced,
- or included in higher-confidence discovery flows.

Recommendations should remain:
- explainable,
- contextual,
- and transparent.

---

# Stage 6 — Long-Term Observation

Technology products evolve over time.

After verification, products may continue to be monitored for:
- durability trends,
- firmware changes,
- compatibility evolution,
- failure patterns,
- and long-term ownership experience.

This stage is important because:
- initial reviews may not reflect long-term reality.

---

# Importance of Long-Term Reliability

Some products perform well initially but degrade later due to:
- wear,
- firmware problems,
- thermal issues,
- or ecosystem abandonment.

Project Trident should eventually incorporate:
- long-term ownership signals,
- and lifecycle-based trust adjustments.

Trust should remain dynamic rather than permanently fixed.

---

# Stage 7 — Re-Evaluation

Products may require re-evaluation after:
- major firmware updates,
- hardware revisions,
- compatibility changes,
- or significant reliability reports.

The platform should acknowledge that:
- technology products are not static.

Recommendations should evolve when:
- evidence changes,
- technology changes,
- or ownership experience changes.

---

# Stage 8 — Archived or Deprecated Status

Older products may eventually become:
- technologically outdated,
- unsupported,
- difficult to recommend,
- or no longer relevant.

Project Trident should clearly indicate when products become:
- deprecated,
- archived,
- or no longer recommended for future-focused buyers.

Historical visibility should remain possible for research purposes.

---

# Fair Visibility Philosophy

The lifecycle system should support:
- innovation visibility,
without:
- misleading trust assumptions.

Project Trident should avoid:
- hiding new technologies,
while also avoiding:
- overpromoting unverified claims.

Balance is essential.

---

# Lifecycle Transparency

Users should always understand:
- what lifecycle stage a product is in,
- what has been verified,
- and what evidence currently exists.

Lifecycle visibility helps users:
- interpret recommendations correctly,
- understand uncertainty,
- and make more informed decisions.

---

# Long-Term Product Ecosystem Vision

As Project Trident evolves, the lifecycle system may eventually support:
- vendor transparency systems,
- reviewer workflows,
- automated monitoring,
- and structured reliability tracking.

However, trust should remain:
- explainable,
- transparent,
- and evidence-driven.

---

# Anti-Goals

The product lifecycle system should avoid:
- artificial hype,
- premature trust assignment,
- opaque verification behavior,
- and popularity-only visibility systems.

The platform should never imply certainty where certainty does not exist.

---

# Final Principle

> Product visibility and product trust are not the same thing.

Project Trident exists to help users understand that distinction clearly.
