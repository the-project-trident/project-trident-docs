# MVP Scope

# Project Trident MVP Scope Definition

---

# Introduction

The Project Trident MVP (Minimum Viable Product) is intended to validate:
- platform philosophy,
- trust systems,
- educational discovery,
- and technology-first recommendation workflows.

The MVP should remain:
- focused,
- narrow,
- understandable,
- and operationally manageable.

The objective is not to:
- build a massive platform immediately,
- maximize feature count,
- or compete with large marketplaces during the first phase.

The objective is to:
- validate whether trust-oriented technology discovery creates real user value.

---

# Core MVP Philosophy

Project Trident believes that:
> a smaller, clearer, and more trustworthy MVP is more valuable than a large but unfocused product.

The MVP should optimize for:
- clarity,
- trust,
- explainability,
- and learning.

The platform should avoid:
- unnecessary complexity,
- feature overload,
- and premature scaling behavior.

---

# Primary MVP Goals

The MVP should validate:

- technology-first discovery,
- trust-oriented recommendations,
- educational exploration,
- verification workflows,
- and comparison-based decision support.

The MVP should help answer:
- Do users value transparent technology understanding?
- Do users trust contextual recommendations?
- Does educational discovery improve decision-making?
- Can trust systems remain understandable?

---

# Initial Product Scope

The MVP should intentionally focus on:
- a narrow hardware category.

Suggested initial focus:
- Controllers
- Gaming input devices
- Hall Effect technologies
- Drift-resistant hardware

This narrow scope improves:
- consistency,
- methodology quality,
- and operational focus.

---

# Why Narrow Scope Matters

A focused MVP allows:
- deeper product understanding,
- stronger trust systems,
- and better educational quality.

The platform should first prove:
- its philosophy,
- workflows,
- and recommendation systems

before:
- expanding into broader categories.

---

# Core MVP Features

The MVP should initially include:

1. Product Pages
2. Technology Education Pages
3. Comparison System
4. Basic Verification Workflow
5. Search & Discovery
6. Trust Visibility
7. Reviewer Operations
8. Recommendation Context

---

# 1. Product Pages

## Purpose

Provide:
- structured product understanding,
- trust visibility,
- and trade-off explanation.

---

## MVP Requirements

Product pages should include:
- product overview
- technology breakdown
- verification status
- trade-off analysis
- recommendation context

---

## Philosophy

Product pages should prioritize:
- understanding,
not:
- conversion pressure.

---

# 2. Technology Education Pages

## Purpose

Help users understand:
- technologies,
- engineering trade-offs,
- and long-term implications.

---

## Example Topics

- Hall Effect systems
- Drift resistance
- Input latency
- Wireless technologies
- Repairability

---

## Importance

Educational systems are one of the MVP’s biggest differentiators.

---

# 3. Comparison System

## Purpose

Allow users to:
- compare products,
- understand trade-offs,
- and evaluate technologies contextually.

---

## MVP Focus

Comparisons should emphasize:
- explanation,
- and transparency.

Not:
- simplistic ranking behavior.

---

# 4. Basic Verification Workflow

## Purpose

Establish:
- trust systems,
- testing workflows,
- and evidence management.

---

## MVP Requirements

The MVP should support:
- verification states
- evidence tracking
- reviewer notes
- trust visibility

---

## Important Principle

Trust systems should remain:
- explainable,
- and transparent.

---

# 5. Search & Discovery

## Purpose

Allow users to:
- discover products,
- explore technologies,
- and navigate educational content.

---

## MVP Focus

Search should prioritize:
- understanding-first exploration.

Not:
- engagement optimization.

---

# 6. Trust Visibility

## Purpose

Clearly communicate:
- verification status,
- recommendation confidence,
- and uncertainty levels.

---

## Example States

- Verified
- Under Observation
- Limited Long-Term Data
- Emerging Technology

---

## Importance

Trust visibility is foundational to platform identity.

---

# 7. Reviewer Operations

## Purpose

Support:
- structured evaluations,
- reviewer accountability,
- and operational consistency.

---

## MVP Requirements

Reviewer systems should support:
- review workflows
- evidence management
- lifecycle tracking
- recommendation reasoning

---

# 8. Recommendation Context

## Purpose

Explain:
- why products appear,
- what trade-offs exist,
- and what user profiles fit best.

---

## Philosophy

Recommendations should remain:
- contextual,
- explainable,
- and trust-aware.

---

# Features Explicitly Excluded from MVP

The MVP should intentionally avoid:

- large-scale marketplace systems
- advanced personalization
- complex social systems
- large vendor ecosystems
- aggressive monetization systems
- large-scale automation
- excessive AI complexity
- deep distributed infrastructure

---

# Why Exclusions Matter

Removing unnecessary complexity improves:
- execution speed,
- clarity,
- maintainability,
- and learning quality.

Focus is a strategic advantage.

---

# MVP Technical Philosophy

The MVP should prioritize:
- maintainability,
- operational simplicity,
- and rapid iteration.

Examples:
- modular monolith architecture (backend)
- **Angular 17** SPA (or SSR where needed) with **Angular Material 17**
- managed infrastructure
- simpler operational tooling

Frontend decisions: [ADR-005](../08-decisions/ADR-005-angular-frontend-choice.md), [Design System](../04-ui-ux/07-design-system.md), [Implementation Plan](../04-ui-ux/08-frontend-implementation-plan.md).

The platform should avoid:
- premature microservices,
- infrastructure over-engineering,
- and un-themed default Material / marketplace UI patterns.

---

# MVP UX Philosophy

The MVP UX should prioritize:
- calmness,
- readability,
- educational flow,
- and transparency.

The platform should avoid:
- marketplace clutter,
- addictive engagement systems,
- and manipulative UX behavior.

**V1 navbar and footer:** defined in [Navigation Flow — V1 Global Header](../04-ui-ux/02-navigation-flow.md#v1-global-header-navbar).

**V1 UI implementation:** Angular Material per [Design System](../04-ui-ux/07-design-system.md).

---

# MVP Success Metrics

Success should NOT be defined primarily by:
- traffic,
- engagement addiction,
- or rapid monetization.

Success should instead measure:

- trust quality
- recommendation clarity
- educational usefulness
- methodology consistency
- operational maintainability
- user understanding improvement

---

# MVP Learning Goals

The MVP should help the platform learn:

- what users value most,
- what creates trust,
- what creates confusion,
- what workflows fail,
- and what methodologies need refinement.

Learning quality matters more than:
- launch scale.

---

# Long-Term MVP Importance

The MVP establishes:
- architectural direction,
- trust philosophy,
- workflow foundations,
- and platform identity.

Bad MVP decisions become:
- long-term platform problems.

Therefore:
- the MVP should remain highly intentional.

---

# Anti-Goals

The MVP should avoid becoming:
- a marketplace clone,
- an engagement-optimized recommendation engine,
- or a feature-heavy platform with weak trust systems.

If complexity reduces:
- trust,
- explainability,
- or maintainability,

the MVP risks failing its actual purpose.

---

# Final Principle

> The MVP should validate trust, educational discovery, and explainable recommendations — not maximize feature count or short-term growth.
