# Product Page Wireframe

# Project Trident Product Detail Page Structure

---

# Introduction

The product detail page is the most important trust interaction surface within Project Trident.

Its purpose is not simply to:
- display specifications,
- maximize conversions,
- or imitate marketplace product pages.

Its primary purpose is to help users:
- understand the product,
- evaluate technologies,
- interpret trade-offs,
- and assess long-term value transparently.

The page should function as:
- a technology understanding surface,
- a trust surface,
- and a decision support system.

---

# Core Product Page Philosophy

The product page should help users answer:

- Why does this product exist?
- What technologies does it use?
- What trade-offs exist?
- What has been verified?
- What uncertainty remains?
- Who is this product suitable for?

The page should prioritize:
- understanding before persuasion.

---

# Emotional UX Direction

The product page should feel:
- analytical,
- calm,
- transparent,
- and trustworthy.

It should avoid:
- marketplace-style urgency,
- aggressive purchasing pressure,
- and exaggerated recommendation behavior.

The objective is:
- informed confidence,
not:
- impulsive conversion.

---

# High-Level Product Page Structure

The page is organized into:

1. Product Overview
2. Trust Layer Summary
3. Technology Breakdown
4. Platform Verification
5. Trade-Off Analysis
6. Long-Term Ownership Considerations
7. Comparison Opportunities
8. Recommendation Context
9. Transparency & Methodology
10. Community & Observation Signals

---

# Section 1 — Product Overview

## Purpose

Provide a structured introduction to the product.

---

## Example Information

- Product name
- Category
- Core technologies
- Compatibility summary
- Product lifecycle status
- Verification status

---

## Important Rule

The overview should remain:
- informational,
not:
- promotional.

Marketing language should be minimized.

---

# Section 2 — Trust Layer Summary

## Purpose

Visually separate trust sources.

---

## Three Trust Layers

### Vendor Claims
Vendor-provided specifications and claims.

### User Feedback
Long-term ownership experiences.

### Platform Verification
Independent evaluation performed by Project Trident.

---

## UX Importance

Users should immediately understand:
- what information comes from where.

This separation is one of the platform’s most important trust principles.

---

# Section 3 — Technology Breakdown

## Purpose

Explain the technologies used within the product.

---

## Example Areas

- Hall Effect inputs
- Polling systems
- Firmware architecture
- Wireless technologies
- Repairability design

---

## UX Goal

Users should understand:
- why technologies matter,
- and how they affect long-term ownership.

The platform should teach, not only display.

---

# Section 4 — Platform Verification

## Purpose

Present independent evaluation results.

---

## Example Evaluation Areas

- Build quality
- Drift resistance
- Input consistency
- Latency
- Firmware quality
- Compatibility reliability
- Long-term durability indicators

---

## Important Principle

Verification results should remain:
- transparent,
- explainable,
- and methodology-driven.

Scores should never appear as:
- unexplained authority signals.

---

# Section 5 — Trade-Off Analysis

## Purpose

Explain strengths and weaknesses honestly.

---

## Example Questions

- What does this product do well?
- What compromises exist?
- What user profiles fit best?
- What risks exist?

---

## Philosophy

No product should appear:
- universally perfect.

Trade-offs should remain highly visible.

---

# Section 6 — Long-Term Ownership Considerations

## Purpose

Help users think beyond:
- first impressions,
- and launch hype.

---

## Example Areas

- Durability expectations
- Firmware support quality
- Repairability
- Ecosystem stability
- Long-term compatibility

---

## UX Goal

Support:
- future-proof decision-making.

---

# Section 7 — Comparison Opportunities

## Purpose

Allow contextual comparison with:
- similar products,
- and competing technologies.

---

## Comparison Focus

Comparisons should emphasize:
- technology differences,
- trade-offs,
- and suitability

rather than:
- simplistic winner declarations.

---

# Section 8 — Recommendation Context

## Purpose

Explain:
- why the product may or may not be recommended.

---

## Example Recommendation Types

- Recommended for durability-focused users
- Strong technology but limited ecosystem maturity
- Good future-proofing with moderate uncertainty

---

## Important Rule

Recommendations should always remain:
- contextual,
- nuanced,
- and explainable.

---

# Section 9 — Transparency & Methodology

## Purpose

Allow users to inspect:
- methodology,
- verification scope,
- and evaluation reasoning.

---

## Example Links

- Review methodology
- Testing process
- Trust principles
- Conflict of interest policy

---

## UX Importance

Trust-related information should remain:
- accessible,
- visible,
- and structurally integrated.

---

# Section 10 — Community & Observation Signals

## Purpose

Surface:
- long-term ownership patterns,
- and evolving ecosystem observations.

---

## Example Areas

- Reliability trends
- Firmware evolution
- Known long-term issues
- Ownership feedback patterns

---

## Philosophy

Long-term observation should remain distinct from:
- launch impressions.

---

# Product Lifecycle Visibility

The product page should clearly communicate:
- lifecycle stage,
- verification maturity,
- and confidence level.

Examples:
- Under Evaluation
- Partially Verified
- Verified
- Long-Term Observation Active

Users should always understand:
- trust maturity.

---

# Information Hierarchy Philosophy

The page should guide users through:

1. Understanding
2. Verification
3. Trade-offs
4. Long-term thinking
5. Decision support

The page should avoid:
- overwhelming users with raw specifications alone.

---

# Visual Design Philosophy

The page should prioritize:
- readability,
- spacing,
- structured sections,
- and information clarity.

The UX should avoid:
- clutter,
- excessive product emphasis,
- and marketplace-style distraction patterns.

---

# Long-Term Product Page Vision

As Project Trident evolves, product pages may eventually support:
- historical reliability tracking,
- dynamic trust updates,
- structured benchmarks,
- and adaptive recommendation systems.

However, the page should always remain:
- explainable,
- transparent,
- and trust-oriented.

---

# Anti-Goals

The product page should avoid becoming:
- a sales page,
- an advertisement surface,
- or a hype-driven recommendation interface.

If the page starts prioritizing:
- conversion,
- engagement metrics,
- or monetization visibility

over:
- understanding and trust,

the platform risks losing credibility.

---

# Final Principle

> A product page should help users understand what they are buying — not pressure them into buying it quickly.
