# ADR-001 — Technology-First Discovery Approach

# Status

Accepted

---

# Date

2026-05-17

---

# Context

Most modern product discovery systems are heavily influenced by:
- popularity,
- advertising,
- engagement optimization,
- marketplace momentum,
- and historical rating accumulation.

This creates several major problems:

- older products dominate visibility,
- newer technologies struggle to gain exposure,
- users make decisions based on popularity rather than understanding,
- and engineering trade-offs become difficult to evaluate clearly.

Traditional marketplaces optimize primarily for:
- conversion,
- engagement,
- and sales velocity.

Project Trident is intended to function differently.

The platform aims to:
- help users understand technologies,
- evaluate trade-offs,
- and make more informed long-term decisions.

This requires a fundamentally different discovery philosophy.

---

# Decision

Project Trident will adopt a:
- technology-first discovery approach.

The platform will prioritize:
- technology understanding,
- engineering trade-offs,
- verification visibility,
- and contextual recommendations

over:
- popularity-driven product surfacing.

Discovery systems should help users explore:
- technologies,
- ownership priorities,
- and long-term implications

rather than:
- simply exposing the most visible products.

Technologies will exist as:
- first-class platform entities,
not:
- hidden metadata only.

---

# Implications

This decision influences:

- search system design,
- recommendation architecture,
- product page structure,
- comparison systems,
- educational systems,
- and long-term platform identity.

The platform will emphasize:
- educational discovery,
- explainable recommendations,
- and structured technology understanding.

This also means:
- newer technologies may receive visibility even before mainstream popularity exists,
provided:
- trust visibility and uncertainty communication remain clear.

---

# Benefits

Potential benefits include:

- better technology understanding,
- improved trade-off awareness,
- fairer visibility for innovation,
- reduced marketplace momentum bias,
- and stronger long-term platform differentiation.

The platform becomes:
- knowledge-oriented,
rather than:
- marketplace-oriented.

---

# Risks

Potential risks include:

- slower user onboarding,
- higher educational complexity,
- reduced short-term engagement metrics,
- and increased recommendation system complexity.

Some users may initially expect:
- traditional marketplace behavior.

Educational UX and transparency become critical.

---

# Alternatives Considered

## Traditional Marketplace Discovery

Prioritize:
- popularity,
- reviews,
- and engagement metrics.

Rejected because:
- it reinforces marketplace momentum problems,
- weakens educational value,
- and reduces platform differentiation.

---

## Pure Expert-Curated Discovery

Rely entirely on:
- reviewer authority,
- and manually curated recommendations.

Rejected because:
- it risks becoming overly centralized,
- difficult to scale,
- and insufficiently explainable.

---

## Algorithmic Personalization First

Prioritize:
- behavioral personalization,
- and recommendation optimization.

Rejected because:
- it risks creating opaque discovery systems,
- weakens transparency,
- and may reduce user understanding.

---

# Long-Term Consequences

This decision strongly shapes:
- platform identity,
- trust systems,
- and user expectations.

Project Trident will likely evolve into:
- a technology intelligence platform,
rather than:
- a conventional recommendation marketplace.

This creates:
- stronger long-term differentiation,
but also:
- higher responsibility for educational clarity and trust consistency.

---

# Final Principle

> Discovery should help users understand technologies and trade-offs more clearly rather than simply expose the most popular products.
