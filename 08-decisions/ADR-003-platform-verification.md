# ADR-003 — Platform Verification System

# Status

Accepted

---

# Date

2026-05-17

---

# Context

Modern product ecosystems are heavily influenced by:
- marketing claims,
- inconsistent reviews,
- popularity momentum,
- and fragmented technical understanding.

Users often struggle to determine:
- what is independently verified,
- what is vendor-provided,
- and what reflects real long-term ownership experience.

Traditional marketplaces typically merge:
- marketing information,
- user sentiment,
- and recommendation visibility

into:
- opaque ranking systems.

This weakens:
- transparency,
- trust clarity,
- and explainable decision-making.

Project Trident is intended to function as:
- a trust-oriented technology intelligence platform.

To support this vision, the platform requires:
- structured verification systems,
- trust visibility,
- and explainable evidence workflows.

---

# Decision

Project Trident will implement:
- a platform verification system.

The platform will explicitly separate:

1. Vendor Claims
2. User Feedback
3. Platform Verification

Verification systems will operate independently from:
- vendor marketing,
- popularity metrics,
- and monetization systems.

Verification workflows should include:
- structured evaluation,
- evidence tracking,
- confidence visibility,
- and long-term observation processes.

Verification status should remain:
- visible,
- explainable,
- and historically traceable.

---

# Verification Philosophy

Verification is not intended to:
- create artificial authority,
- imply absolute certainty,
- or eliminate uncertainty completely.

The purpose of verification is to:
- independently evaluate observable claims,
- improve recommendation clarity,
- and communicate evidence transparently.

Project Trident believes that:
> trustworthy systems explain both what is known and what remains uncertain.

---

# Verification States

The platform may eventually support states such as:

- Under Evaluation
- Partially Verified
- Verified
- Under Observation
- Verification Incomplete
- Limited Long-Term Data

These states help communicate:
- evidence maturity,
- and confidence levels.

---

# Implications

This decision strongly affects:

- product page structure,
- recommendation systems,
- reviewer workflows,
- search systems,
- trust architecture,
- and operational tooling.

The platform requires:
- evidence management systems,
- reviewer accountability infrastructure,
- auditability mechanisms,
- and long-term reliability observation workflows.

---

# Benefits

Potential benefits include:

- stronger trust clarity,
- improved recommendation transparency,
- better long-term credibility,
- reduced marketing confusion,
- and explainable recommendation systems.

Users gain clearer understanding of:
- what has actually been independently evaluated.

---

# Risks

Potential risks include:

- increased operational complexity,
- slower scaling,
- higher reviewer workload,
- and more demanding methodology requirements.

Verification systems require:
- long-term discipline,
- and operational consistency.

---

# Alternatives Considered

## Vendor-Driven Trust Systems

Rely primarily on:
- vendor-provided information,
- and manufacturer claims.

Rejected because:
- vendor incentives do not always align with:
  - transparent trade-off communication,
  - or long-term trust quality.

---

## User Review Aggregation Only

Depend mostly on:
- community reviews,
- ratings,
- and crowd sentiment.

Rejected because:
- popularity signals often lack:
  - technical depth,
  - verification consistency,
  - and contextual understanding.

---

## Fully Automated Scoring Systems

Use:
- algorithmic trust systems,
- and automated evaluation logic only.

Rejected because:
- opaque automation weakens:
  - explainability,
  - accountability,
  - and trust transparency.

Human oversight remains important.

---

# Long-Term Consequences

This decision strongly shapes:
- platform credibility,
- operational architecture,
- reviewer systems,
- and recommendation identity.

Project Trident evolves toward:
- a verification-oriented trust platform,
rather than:
- a popularity-driven recommendation engine.

This creates:
- stronger long-term differentiation,
but also:
- higher responsibility for operational transparency and consistency.

---

# Enforcement Principles

To preserve this decision:
- verification workflows should remain auditable,
- evidence should remain traceable,
- and recommendation systems should remain explainable.

Verification systems should never become:
- opaque authority systems.

Users should always understand:
- what evidence exists,
- what remains uncertain,
- and how conclusions were reached.

---

# Final Principle

> Verification systems should help users distinguish between marketing claims, ownership signals, and independently evaluated evidence in a transparent and explainable way.
